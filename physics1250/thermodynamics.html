Ideal Gas Law

With an Ideal Gas in a container, the pressure in the container times it’s volume is equal to a constant. This constant represents the average kinetic energy in the gas, or the amount of moles and the temperature. But we need one more thing and this is the Ideal Gas Constant (\(R\)) to actually get the equation to work out. The Ideal Gas Constant is equal to \(8.31 \frac{J}{mol \cdot K}\). Therefore the Ideal Gas Law is:

The Ideal Gas Law:

\begin{align*} P \cdot V = nRT \end{align*}

With this equation, we can also set up some equations to determine transformations in pressure, volume amd temperature such as:

\begin{align*} P_1 \cdot V_1 = P_2 \cdot V_2 \end{align*}

and with temperature:

\begin{align*} & \frac{P_1 \cdot V_1}{T_1} = \frac{P_2 \cdot V_2}{T_2} \end{align*}

Specific Heat

The idea of Specific Heat is essentially just that some materials are harder to change the heat of than others. As such every material has its own specific heat (\(c\)).

In order to change the temperature of something, we need to know the amount by which we want the temperature to change, the specific heat of the material, and its mass. So our equation for Heat (\(Q\)) is:

\begin{align*} Q = m c \Delta T \end{align*}

This equation is useful because in a system, the change in heat will always be 0 unless there is an outside force. So that mean if we add up all the Heats (\(Q\)) they should be zero.

\begin{align*} Q_1 + Q_2 + Q_3 + ... Q_n = 0 \end{align*}

Latent Heat   toreview

Whenever a Phase change occurs, or when a material changes state of matter we can’t use our equation for heat. Instead we need to use a new equation for heat using Latent Heat (\(L\)) instead of Specific Heat.

\begin{align*} Q = m L \end{align*}

Then we must use this equation until the material is into the next state of matter where we can use our normal equa ,tion for heat.

Laws of Thermodynamics

Macrostates and Microstates

The values that we’ve been using so far like Pressure, Volume and Temperature are all examples of Macrostates. These are measurable values that we can observe.

However, they aren’t the whole picture. There are another collection of values call Microstates. These have to do with atomic values that we can’t see or measure. Because of this there are many, many Microstates for every Macrostate.

Because of this, the Macrostates of something are only well defined when the system is in equilibrium or when the Macrostates are no longer changing. However, Microstates are always well defined, but extremely hard to measure.

Quasistatic and Reversible Processes

A Quasistatic process is one that it almost Static meaning that if may change but at an extremely slow rate.

Often the terms Quasistatic and Reversible are used interchangeably, however there is no such thing as a Reversible Process in the real world.

These allow us to define our Macrostates for each state in-between the ones we know.

The First Law of Thermodynamics

The First Law of Thermodynamics states that energy cannot be created nor destroyed. It can only be transformed from one form to another.

This means that with Thermodynamics we can track Internal Energy (\(U\) or \(E_{int}\)) of a system. This is the total amount of energy in a system. It is equal to the heat added to the system minus the work done to it.

Work done by the system:

\begin{align*} \Delta U = Q - W \end{align*}

or

Work done to the system:

\begin{align*} \Delta U = Q + W \end{align*}

Isothermic Process

An Isothermic process is one where the temperature of a system remains the same.

  • When Temperature is lower, so is pressure

The work done in an isothermal process is equal to the area under the curve. So if we write the Ideal Gas Law in terms of pressure, we get:

\begin{align*} P = \frac{ n R T }{V} \end{align*}

Now, to get the area under the curve we take the integral from our starting volume to our ending one.

\begin{align*} Work&=\int_{V_1}^{V_2} \frac{n R T}{V}\\ &=n R T \int_{V_1}^{V_2} \frac{1}{V}\\ &=n R T [\ln{V}]_{v_1}^{v_2}\\ &=n R T (\ln{V_2} - \ln{V_1})\\ &=n R T \ln{(\frac{V_2}{V_1})} \end{align*}

This is where our equation for the work of an isothermic system comes from.

Second Law of Thermodynamics

The Second Law of Thermodynamics states that the change in entropy of the universe is always greater than or equal to \(0\). Meaning that the universe can never loose entropy.

  • One of the side effects of this law is that heat only moves one way, from hot to cold. So hot things can only loose heat and cold things can only gain it.

Carnot Cycle

A Carnot Cycle is a cycle that goes between two different temperatures. The most important part of the Carnot Cycle is that it’s reversible. Meaning that

  • The ratio between the volumes at state A and B is equal to the ratio between states C and D. \(\frac{V_B}{V_A} = \frac{V_C}{V_D}\).

Entropy

Entropy (\(S\)) is a valid state variable in a Carnot Cycle because in total it doesn’t change (\(\Delta S = 0\)). It represents how many more microstates a system can take one.

  • It’s defined as \(S = \frac{Q}{T}\). However, this is only its definition in a reversible system.
  • In an irreversible reaction Entropy is no longer a state variable, we have to make up a reversible system to find Entropy.
  • Entropy can also be defined as \(S = N k_b ln(\Omega)\). Where \(\Omega\) is the number of states.
