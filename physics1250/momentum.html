Momentum

Linear Momentum and Force

Linear Momentum is the product of a system’s mass and its velocity, \(P = m * v\). Momentum and Force are closely related, because a force acting over a time can change the momentum of an object.

Newton’s Second Law of Motion was actually first defined in terms of momentum, where \(F_{net} = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t}\).

Impulse

The change in momentum over a time is called Impulse. This value can be found be rearranging the equation \(F_{net} = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t}\), into \(\Delta p = F_{net} * \Delta t\).

  • A common example for impulse is that of seatbelts. What a seatbelt is doing is decreasing the impulse of a collision on your body by increasing the time it takes.

Conservation of Momentum

Momentum is a useful quality because it is conserved. This means that when two systems get into a collision, we can use momentum to analyze them. The Conservation of momentum is defined as \(\overrightarrow{P_i} + I = \overrightarrow{P_f}\). Where both momenta can be vectors.

Elastic and Inelastic

There are two different types of collisions, Elastic and Inelastic. The main difference between the two being if they conserve their Internal Kinetic Energy or not.

Elastic

An Elastic Collision is one where the objects essentially bounce off of each other and head in opposite directions after a collision. During an Elastic Collision, Internal Kinetic Energy is conserved.

  • Because Total Kinetic Energy is conserved we know that \(p_{1i} + p_{2i} = p_{1f} + p_{2f}\) and \(K_{1i} + K_{2i} = K_{1f} + K_{2f}\)

Inelastic

An Inelastic Collision happens when some other force takes energy out of the system during the collision. Because of this Internal Kinetic Energy is not conserved.

  • A Perfectly Inelastic Collision is one where the objects are stuck together after the collision.
