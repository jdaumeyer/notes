Cognition

Our cognitive processes take place in 4 different phases: attention, encoding/perception, storage and retrieval.

Attention
Is how we direct our concentration. It becomes easier with clear goals and information being visible in our environment.
Encoding/Perception
Is how we take sensory information and turn it into experiences
Storage
When we move information into memory. We filter this information before committing it to memory.
Retrieval
The fetching of information that is already in our memory.

Performance Load

Kinematic Load
The amount of effort required by the user’s body.
  • Operability
Sensory Load
The amount of effort required from the senses of a user.
  • Perceptability
Cognitive Load
The amount of cognition that a user needs.
  • Understandability

Designing for Perception

Focus on making information clear and organized

Type

Legibility
How clear individual letters are.
Readability
The ease of which words can be read.
Contrast
The difference between some text and its background. This is something we want to maximize.
Spacing
We want as much space as possible without impacting readability.
Horror Vacui
The fear of blank space, some people are scared of large amounts of blank space.
Alignment
For English we prefer left alignment, centering can also look good so the goal is to keep alignment consistent. Justification should be avoided.
Kerning
The spacing between individual letters

Organizing Information

Wabi Sabi
if something is perfect, it makes it less beautiful.
Signal to Noise Ratio
The ratio of relevant to irrelevant information. Anything not related to the signal we want should be cut down as much as possible.
80/20 Rule
80% of use comes from 20% of the features of a design.
Paradox of Choice
The more choice people are presented with, the less satisfied they’ll be with the choice they make.
Garbage In, Garbage Out
The more freedom for input, the less specific the response will be.
Hierarchy
Make the most important things the most prominent.
Progressive Disclosure
Show only a small section of information at a time and allow users to choose to see more.

Designing for Attention

With attention, the biggest thing we want to avoid is sensory overload.

  • Things higher and more centered seem more important.
  • Bright colors draw attention but contrast is more important.
  • Movement prays on our instincts and we are naturally drawn to them.

Distractions

Visual, auditory, kinematic and psychological stimulus can all create distractions.

Inability to process
We don’t want to just grab attention, we want to turn that attention into something.
Multitasking
Humans can’t have their attention on more than one thing at once. Instead we end up task switching.

Designing for Memory

Consistency is key for memorability. Context is helpful since our memory is relative and context will help fill any gaps.

  • Prioritize recognition over recall to take the load off of users.

Organization

The organization of information is great for memory. However different users require different methods of organization.

To cover our bases with organization, we use LATCH (5 hat racks):

  • Location
  • Alphabetic
  • Time
  • Categorical
  • Hierarchical

Data Visualization takes complicated information and makes it more digestible pictorially.
